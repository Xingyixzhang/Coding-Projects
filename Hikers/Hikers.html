Trail Types

Foot Trails

This is the typical type of trails that come to people's mind when
we talk about hiking. Varying from place to place, the distance,
incline, terrain, and difficulty can differ for this type of trails.

Bikeways

Also called Bike Paths, are trails that allow bicyclists
(Some may be multi-use before giving way to bike or foot-only
specific paths). For those multi-use trails, be sure to
always practice good hiking etiquette and know your right-of-way.

Boardwalks

Boardwalks are walkways that cross over water or marshy ground.

Typically, they are made of wood or other smooth materials and accessible to wheelchairs. We can normally find them close to parking lots, so do expect larger crowds. Special Note: Stay on boardways to protect native plants and animals.

Interpretive/Nature Trails

These trails tell a story.
While you are on the trail, you may find signs on the path sharing information
about the world around you, what it used to be, how it becomes what it is now,
and what's to hope in the future.
Certain trails also include statues to help paint the scene.

Multi-Use Trails

Hikers, bicyclists, and horses share the dirt on these multi-use trails.
Know your right of way! Be mindful of trail etiquette. Bikes and
horses can use some trails and roads in parks but be sure to check for signs.

Hiking OffRoad

These off-trail areas in the wilderness of national parks
will give you a more rugged experience. Check with the park
to ensure you obtain permits,
follow wilderness rules, and leave No Trace Principles.
